Fiscal PolicyGovernment taxing & spending
Who Controls It
The President proposes a federal budget each year — but that is all the President can do on their own. For anything to actually happen, both houses of Congress must agree and pass it into law: first the House of Representatives (435 members), then the Senate (100 members). Because this process requires debate, compromise, and votes in two chambers, fiscal policy is slow — it can take months or years, even during a crisis.
TOOL 1Government Spending — Congress can increase or decrease spending on things like infrastructure, schools, defense, or social programs (e.g. unemployment benefits).
TOOL 2Taxes — Congress can raise or lower taxes on individuals and businesses, changing how much money people have available to spend.
Expansionary (stimulate): Increase spending OR cut taxes → puts more money into the economy.
Contractionary (slow down): Cut spending OR raise taxes → removes money from the economy.
Monetary PolicyControlling money & interest rates
What Is the Federal Reserve?
The Federal Reserve (called "the Fed") is the central bank of the United States. Think of it as the bank that all other banks answer to. Congress created it in 1913 to manage the nation's money supply and keep the economy stable. The Fed is independent — it is not controlled by the President or Congress, and its leaders are not elected by voters. This is intentional: economists believe that decisions about interest rates should be made by financial experts free from political pressure. The Fed's board of governors meets roughly 8 times per year and can act far faster than Congress.
TOOL 1Interest Rates — the Fed raises or lowers the cost of borrowing money. Lower rates → cheaper loans → more spending & investment. Higher rates → expensive loans → less spending.
TOOL 2Money Supply — the Fed can increase or decrease the amount of money circulating in the economy by buying or selling government bonds.
Expansionary (stimulate): Lower interest rates OR increase money supply → cheaper/easier to borrow.
Contractionary (slow down): Raise interest rates OR decrease money supply → more expensive to borrow, less spending.

📊 Expert Analysis — Recession

What Congress Would Likely Do

Most economists recommend expansionary fiscal policy during a recession. Congress would likely pass a stimulus package — increasing spending on infrastructure, unemployment benefits, and direct payments — and might also cut taxes temporarily to put more money in consumers' hands. The goal: inject money into the economy to boost demand and employment. The risk: adding to the national debt.

What the Fed Would Likely Do

The Fed would move to expansionary monetary policy — cutting interest rates to make borrowing cheaper for businesses and consumers. They might also increase the money supply by purchasing government bonds ("quantitative easing"). Lower rates encourage businesses to invest and hire. The risk: if rates stay too low too long, inflation can follow.

Working Together: During the 2008 financial crisis and COVID recession, Congress passed massive spending bills while the Fed slashed rates to near zero. Fiscal AND monetary expansion working together helped prevent complete economic collapse. This coordination is rare but powerful.

📊 Expert Analysis — Inflation

What Congress Would Likely Do

To fight inflation, Congress would turn to contractionary fiscal policy — cutting government spending and possibly raising taxes to remove money from the economy. Less spending means less demand, which puts downward pressure on prices. In practice this is politically very difficult — cutting popular programs or raising taxes rarely wins votes, and Congress often moves slowly.

What the Fed Would Likely Do

The Fed's primary tool against inflation is raising interest rates. Higher rates make borrowing more expensive, discouraging spending and investment — cooling demand. The Fed may also decrease the money supply. This is exactly what happened in 2022–2023 when inflation hit 9%; the Fed raised rates from near zero to over 5% in about 18 months. The risk: raising rates too aggressively can trigger a recession.

Tension Point: The Fed can act quickly; Congress often can't or won't. During the 2021–2023 inflation surge, the Fed raised rates aggressively while Congress continued high spending — arguably working at cross-purposes. This is a real tension between independent monetary policy and politically-driven fiscal policy.

📊 Expert Analysis — Stagflation

What Congress Would Likely Do

Stagflation is a policy trap. Expansionary fiscal policy could ease unemployment — but would likely worsen inflation. Contractionary policy might cool inflation — but could deepen the recession. Most economists argue there is no clean fiscal solution. Congress might focus on supply-side measures — investing in energy independence or reducing production costs — to address the root cause rather than the symptoms.

What the Fed Would Likely Do

The Fed faces the same impossible trade-off. In the 1970s stagflation crisis, Fed Chair Paul Volcker chose to aggressively raise interest rates — prioritizing inflation over unemployment. It worked, but caused a severe recession and unemployment hit 10%. The Fed essentially decided that runaway inflation was the bigger long-term danger. This decision remains controversial among economists today.

The Hard Truth: Stagflation exposes the limits of both fiscal and monetary policy. Neither set of tools fixes both problems at once. The 1970s taught economists that sometimes there is no good option — only less bad ones. The real question becomes: which problem do you prioritize, and who bears the cost of that choice?
